Section02 / The Brief
FiledApr 2026
TagsHealth · Screen time · Adolescents · Apple ecosystem
StatusResearch complete · Design in progress

The fight starts the same way every afternoon. The phone has been out for hours. A parent says five more minutes. A child says five more minutes. Someone yells. The phone goes back into a pocket. Tomorrow, it happens again.

Apple already ships two of the most powerful behavioral tools on a teenager's phone: Screen Time and Health. One restricts. The other measures. Neither rewards. The result is an ecosystem that knows your child is sedentary, knows exactly which apps they're sedentary on, and does nothing to connect the two.

Our proposal extends Apple Health with a simple inversion: instead of restricting apps with timers, the apps stay locked by default and activity unlocks them. A daily step goal isn't a chore. It's a price tag. Steps are currency. Minutes on Instagram are the product.

It feels like taking any toy away from a kid — there's this tantrumy reaction he has. Interview 02, parent of a 12-year-old

Across two parent interviews, every challenge they described traced back to the same root: the rules feel arbitrary. Screen time is taken away in the moment, by a parent who is themselves on a phone, while a child is mid-game with friends online. The rule isn't predictable. It isn't earned. It isn't fair.

Our design tries to make the rule into a system. Once a parent and child set the activity-to-app exchange together, the daily negotiation moves out of the dinner table and into a transparent dashboard. The argument doesn't disappear, but it shifts: from can I have my phone to I'll go on a walk first.

04 High-Fidelity Prototypes

The following screens represent the unfinished Earn Your Screen system, an extension of Apple Health that turns daily physical activity into a transparent, negotiated currency for app access. Each flow was designed to serve a distinct moment in the parent-child dynamic: setup, enforcement, negotiation, and reflection.

Rather than a punitive restriction model, every screen is framed around agency and clarity. Kids see exactly what they've earned and what's left over. Parents set expectations once, then step back. The system argues for them.

Design rationale
Transparency first
Every rule is visible to both parent and child. No surprises, no arbitrary enforcement.
Earn, don't plead
Activity unlocks screen time automatically. No asking, no negotiation in the moment.
Fit Apple's language
UI patterns mirror Health and Screen Time so the system feels native, not foreign.
Flow 01 · Critical Path
App Launch Intercept
The moment a child tries to open a restricted app before earning screen time, the system's most visible touchpoint.
Why this screen matters This is the product's core behavior loop. Rather than a blunt lock screen, the intercept shows the child exactly how close they are to earning access: steps completed, steps remaining, and a direct path to go earn the rest. The design deliberately avoids shame or punishment language; instead it presents a clear, achievable transaction. Research from our parent interviews showed that arbitrary restrictions cause the strongest emotional reactions. Making the rule visible and the path forward immediate is the primary design response to that finding.
Flow 03 · Unlock Flow
Move to Unlock
The step-by-step unlock sequence that converts physical activity into earned screen minutes.
Why this matters The unlock screen borrows the ritual of Apple Pay, a deliberate, satisfying micro-moment that makes earning feel real. Progress is shown in physical terms (steps, distance) rather than abstract time, grounding the reward in tangible effort. The visual design mirrors Apple Health's ring metaphor so the behavior feels continuous with what the OS already teaches.
Flow 05 · Negotiation
Parent ↔ Child Approval Loop
When a child needs an exception, sick day or school event, they request it and a parent approves or denies.
Why this matters No system survives contact with real life without an escape valve. The approval loop preserves parental authority for genuine exceptions without making the exception the norm. Crucially, the request comes from the child: they initiate the conversation rather than the parent making a unilateral call. This keeps the dynamic collaborative and teaches kids to articulate their needs rather than react emotionally.
Flow 06 · Reflection
Daily Summary & Streaks
End-of-day recap showing activity completed, screen time earned, and streak progress toward longer-term goals.
Why this screen matters Behavioral change research consistently shows that feedback loops (seeing your progress) are as important as the incentive itself. The Daily Summary closes the loop: it makes the day's effort legible and celebrates it. Streaks extend the time horizon beyond a single day, creating a longer-term investment that makes kids less likely to break the habit. For parents, the summary replaces the nightly interrogation ("did you hit your steps?") with a shared artifact. The conversation moves from accusation to review.

Milestone6, Usability study

We tested with four participants spanning both sides of the parent-child relationship. Mom 1 (51) was a returning interviewee, mother of a screen-time-heavy teen basketball player. Little Brother (14) is a younger sibling of a team member and represents our primary user. Mom 2 (41), also a returning interviewee, is mother of a 1st grader who gravitates toward outdoor play. Little Sister (7), another sibling, represents a younger child with moderate screen use who loves going outside with family.

Each participant saw Milestone 4 first, then Milestone 5, and completed the same three tasks on each. All four preferred Milestone 5.

Kids responded to ownership

Earning felt better than being blocked.

Little Brother called the M4 unlock screen "actually kinda cool, like you earned it." On M5 the greyed-out overlay landed even stronger: "You can see what you're missing, which makes you actually want to go do the steps." Little Sister liked that it could keep her on a schedule and justify outdoor time with cousins as screen credit.

Parents responded to peace

The system removes them from the argument.

Mom 1 on M4: "It's not me being the bad guy anymore, it's just the system." On M5 the approval flow went further: "He can submit a request and I approve it, it's not a fight." Mom 2 called M5 "simple and modern" and noted she could see herself using it for her 15-year-old as well.

Two things needed fixing

An ambiguous bar and an unguarded edit button.

Little Brother couldn't tell if the M4 progress bar tracked steps, minutes, or something else. Mom 1 flagged that she couldn't tell who controlled the PIN for goal adjustments and worried a child could bypass the system entirely. Both issues pointed to the same gap: the rules existed, but the interface didn't make them legible or enforceable.

Point of view

How fair the system feels to users matters more than how strict it is. Both participants responded most positively to features that reduced friction between parent and child: the approval flow, the progress-visible lock screen, the ability to request more time. When the rules feel objective and the child has some agency within them, both sides feel better about the system.

01
Revised · App Launch Intercept From wall to invitation
Before, Milestone 4
After, Milestone 5
Before · Framing
Leads with "Instagram is Locked"

A punitive declaration at the top of the screen. The locked framing positions the system as a wall rather than a path, and testing showed participants responded defensively before reading anything else.

After · Framing
Leads with "Move a little to unlock Snapchat"

An encouraging action prompt rather than a hard stop. A little is intentional: it reframes the gap as something easily within reach, shifting the emotional register from punishment to challenge before the child even checks their step count.

Before · Progress + Action
Ambiguous bar, two competing buttons

The progress bar showed "5,000 / 10,000" with no explicit unit, so Little Brother couldn't confirm if it was tracking steps, minutes, or something else. View Goals and Ask For More Time then split his attention at the highest-friction moment.

After · Progress + Action
Activity ring, one clear next step

A ring display shows exact steps completed and remaining, plus a contextual hint translating the gap into real-world effort with a preview of the reward. A single Open Health button points to one clear action. Not now is a quiet secondary option, not a competing choice.

Why it changed

The locked app screen was redesigned after users in testing flagged that the progress bar on the original screen was ambiguous and that the overall framing felt more like a punishment than a challenge. The original screen leads with "Instagram is Locked", uses a generic progress bar with minimal context, and splits the child's attention across two competing action buttons at exactly the moment they're already frustrated. The redesigned screen addresses all three of these issues: it opens with an encouraging prompt rather than a hard stop, replaces the ambiguous bar with a ring based display showing exact numbers and steps remaining, adds a contextual hint translating the gap into real world effort with a clear preview of the reward, and consolidates the action buttons into a single button that points the child toward one clear next step. Together these changes transform the locked state from feeling like a wall into feeling like a challenge that's one short walk away from being solved, which is exactly the motivational dynamic the extension is built around.

02
Revised · Goal Management & Approval Flow Closing the bypass loophole
Before, Milestone 4
After, Milestone 5
Before · Control
Child could directly edit and save goals

The original screen gave a child direct access to edit Time Requirements and Activity Goals via the Update Goal button, with nothing stopping them from loosening their own restrictions. Mom 1 flagged this immediately: she couldn't tell who actually controlled the PIN or whether her son could bypass it on his own.

After · Control
Changes route through parent approval

That direct edit access is replaced entirely by a request flow. The child proposes a reward adjustment within a parent-defined range using the slider, and the change is sent to the parent as a notification rather than applied immediately. No change takes effect without parental approval.

Before · Bounds
No guardrails on what a child could change

Without bounded controls, a child could theoretically set their activity goal to zero and screen time to unlimited. The system's rules existed but the interface didn't enforce them structurally.

After · Bounds
Slider bounded by parent-set range

The reward slider runs from a parent-set minimum (15 min) to a maximum (3 hr). Any request the child submits is already within acceptable limits, reducing friction on the parent's end when approving and preventing bad-faith requests.

Why it changed

The Adjust Goal screen was redesigned after user testing revealed that parents couldn't tell who actually controlled goal changes, and worried a child could bypass the system on their own. In the original screen, a child had direct access to edit time requirements and update the goal themselves via the "Update Goal" button with nothing stopping them from loosening their own restrictions. In the redesigned screen, that direct edit access is replaced entirely by a request flow. The child proposes a reward adjustment within a parent defined range using the slider, and the change is sent to the parent as a notification rather than applied immediately. This preserves parent authority while still giving kids a legitimate voice in the negotiation.
